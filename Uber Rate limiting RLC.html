🤖 RLC — Rate Limit Configurator

The brain that automatically sets & updates rate limits — so humans don't have to

GRL does this

⚡ Enforces limits in real-time

When traffic is flowing, GRL is the cop at the checkpoint — it counts, decides, and drops requests right now.

RLC does this

🧠 Decides what the limits should be

RLC is the analyst working in the background — it looks at weeks of history and writes the rule book that GRL follows.

📖 The problem RLC solves: Uber has thousands of services. Each pair of services needs a rate limit. Engineers used to set these limits manually. The problem? Traffic patterns change constantly — new features ship, seasonal spikes happen, services scale up. A limit set 3 months ago might be way too strict (throttling healthy traffic) or way too loose (offering zero protection). RLC fixes this by automatically recalculating limits based on real traffic history. 👇 Walk through how it works.
1
😩
The old way — humans setting limits manually
The Problem
Before RLC, an engineer had to manually look at dashboards and decide:

"Trip Service gets about 800 req/s from Notification Service on a normal day. Let me set the limit to 1,200 to give 50% headroom."

Sounds reasonable — but 3 months later, Uber launched a new feature. Notification traffic grew to 1,500 req/s normally. Now the limit of 1,200 is throttling healthy traffic. And nobody remembered to update it.
Jan 1: Engineer sets limit = 1,200 req/s ✅ reasonable Mar 1: Normal traffic is now 1,500 req/s (new feature shipped) Limit is still = 1,200 req/s ← now throttling real traffic! Jun 1: Engineer finally notices → updates limit = 2,000 req/s But now it's too loose → offers no protection during a spike! Problem: limits go stale. humans can't keep up with thousands of services.
2
📥
Step 1: RLC collects weeks of traffic history
Data Collection
On a fixed schedule (e.g. every day), RLC goes to Uber's observability platform (think: a giant metrics store) and pulls the actual RPS data for every service pair over the past few weeks.

It's asking: "What did real traffic actually look like?"
RLC asks the metrics store: "Give me the last 3 weeks of RPS for every caller → service pair" Notification → Trip Service : Week 1 peak: 820 req/s Week 2 peak: 950 req/s ← Black Friday spike Week 3 peak: 870 req/s Average peak: 880 req/s Driver App → Trip Service : Average peak: 2,100 req/s ... and so on for ALL service pairs at Uber (thousands of them)
3
🧮
Step 2: RLC computes a safe limit with buffer headroom
Limit Calculation
RLC doesn't just use the average — it uses the historical peak and adds a buffer headroom (e.g. 20–30% extra) to handle natural growth and minor spikes, without going so high that the limit becomes meaningless.

This is the core formula: New Limit = Peak Traffic × (1 + Buffer %)
For Notification → Trip Service: Observed peak (last 3 weeks) = 950 req/s Buffer headroom = +30% New safe limit = 950 × 1.30 = 1,235 req/s ✅ This means: • Normal traffic (~880/s) → always passes (well under limit) • Small spikes (~1,100/s) → still passes (within headroom) • Bug/attack (5,000/s) → gets shed (way over limit)
4
🧩
RLC supports 3 different policy types
Flexibility
Not every service is the same. RLC has 3 calculation strategies — teams pick whichever fits their service:
📈
Historical Peak
Default. Uses past N weeks of real traffic + buffer. Great for most services.
🔮
Predictive / Forecast
Uses traffic forecasts + planned capacity. Used by Maps & Location — they know future load in advance.
📌
Fixed Quota
A hard static limit per caller. Used when you want strict fairness (e.g. partner APIs).
5
🚀
Step 3: RLC writes & pushes the new limits to GRL
Deployment
Once RLC has computed the new limits for all service pairs, it:

1. Writes them to a shared config repository
2. Pushes them into GRL through the existing control plane

GRL's Regional Controller picks them up and distributes them down to all RL Clients in the mesh. No human needed. No deployment. No code change.

🛡️
🧪
Bonus: Shadow Mode — try before you enforce
Safety Net
Teams nervous about activating RLC can run it in Shadow Mode first. RLC generates and monitors the limits — but doesn't actually enforce them yet.

Dashboards show: "If this limit was active, X% of traffic would have been dropped." Teams can verify the limits look sane before flipping the switch to live enforcement.
Shadow Mode ON for Trip Service: RLC computed limit: 1,235 req/s Day 1 simulation: traffic = 880/s → 0% would be dropped ✅ Day 2 simulation: traffic = 910/s → 0% would be dropped ✅ Day 3 simulation: traffic = 870/s → 0% would be dropped ✅ Team is confident → flips to LIVE enforcement ✅ (If shadow showed 20% of legit traffic being dropped → engineer adjusts buffer headroom before going live)
